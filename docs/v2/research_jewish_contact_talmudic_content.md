## 1. THE CLAIM UNDER INVESTIGATION

The hypothesis: Muhammad spent 13 years in Medina (622-632 CE) in close contact with three major Jewish tribes — the Banu Qaynuqa, Banu Nadir, and Banu Qurayza. During this period, he could have learned Talmudic and Midrashic material from Jewish scholars and incorporated it into the Qur'an.

This is one of the oldest and most persistent naturalistic explanations for the Qur'an's engagement with Jewish extra-biblical material. It was first systematically argued by Abraham Geiger in 1833 and has been repeated in various forms ever since.

**This research tests it from every available angle.**

---

## 2. THE JEWISH TRIBES OF MEDINA: WHO WERE THEY?

### 2a. The Three Tribes

| Tribe            | Primary Occupation                       | Status                                                         |
| ---------------- | ---------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Banu Qaynuqa** | Goldsmiths, commerce                     | Owned no agricultural land; craftsmen and traders              |
| **Banu Nadir**   | Agriculture, moneylending, weapons trade | Considerable affluence; commercial relations with Meccan Arabs |
| **Banu Qurayza** | Agriculture, trade                       | Allied with Medinan Arab tribes                                |

### 2b. Language

The Medina Jews spoke **a Jewish dialect of Arabic**. They bore Arabic names and lived according to the cultural norms of their Arab neighbors. They were, by all accounts, deeply **Arabized** — they had been living in the Hijaz for centuries and had assimilated linguistically and culturally to their environment, while maintaining Jewish religious identity and communal exclusivity.

**Key point:** The Talmud was composed in Hebrew and Aramaic in the academies of Babylonia (modern Iraq). The Medina Jews spoke Arabic. This language barrier is not trivial — Talmudic study requires not just passive understanding of Hebrew/Aramaic but active fluency in the technical legal and exegetical terminology of rabbinic discourse.

### 2c. Scholarly Status: Were They Talmudic Scholars?

This is the critical question, and the evidence is ambiguous:

**Evidence FOR some level of religious knowledge:**

- Ibn Hisham mentions a _bayt al-midras_ (house of study) in Medina, visited occasionally by both Muhammad and some of his followers.
- The Jewish communities reportedly "ridiculed" Muhammad and "delighted in drawing him into arguments to expose his ignorance" (Jewish Encyclopedia, s.v. "Medina"), suggesting theological sophistication.
- Some of Muhammad's followers are said to have been former rabbinical scholars.
- Haggai Mazuz has argued that Hijazi Arabian Judaism was rabbinic and halakhic.

**Evidence AGAINST them being Talmudic scholars:**

- Mazuz's thesis has been criticized for "uncritical reliance on traditional sources." The nature of Hijazi Judaism remains controversial.
- The extensive Jewish literary record (Talmud, Geonic literature) **rarely makes mention of Jews living in Arab lands**, and the Talmud's occasional references to "Arabia" mostly mean southern Israel/Jordan, not the Hijaz.
- **No Talmudic academy existed anywhere in Arabia.** The nearest academies were in Sura and Pumbedita in Babylonia (modern Iraq), approximately 1,500 km from Medina.
- There is **no documented connection** between the Medina Jewish communities and the Babylonian Talmudic academies.
- The communities were primarily agricultural, commercial, and artisanal — not scholarly.
- Norman Stillman's comprehensive _Jews of Arab Lands_ focuses on political and economic dimensions; the scholarly infrastructure of Hijazi Judaism is largely undocumented.

**Assessment:** The Medina Jews had religious knowledge sufficient for communal Jewish practice and theological debate. But there is a vast difference between knowing Jewish tradition at the community level and being Talmudic scholars capable of transmitting the detailed content of the Babylonian Talmud, Midrash Rabbah, the Mishnah Sanhedrin, and various Targumim. The evidence for the latter is weak to nonexistent.

## 3. THE TIMELINE PROBLEM: MECCAN VS. MEDINAN CONTENT

### 3a. The Fatal Chronological Objection

This is perhaps the single most devastating problem for the "learned from Medina Jews" hypothesis:

**Most of the Talmudic/Midrashic parallels in the Qur'an appear in MECCAN surahs — revealed BEFORE Muhammad ever migrated to Medina.**

| Qur'anic Passage                                  | Talmudic/Midrashic Parallel               | Surah Classification           | Period                               |
| ------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- | ------------------------------ | ------------------------------------ |
| Abraham smashing idols (21:51-70)                 | Midrash Rabbah Genesis 38:13              | **Surah Al-Anbiya — MECCAN**   | Late Meccan, ~2-3 years before Hijra |
| Abraham smashing idols (37:83-98)                 | Same Midrashic tradition                  | **Surah As-Saffat — MECCAN**   | Meccan                               |
| Abraham and the fire (21:68-70)                   | Talmudic tradition about Nimrod           | **Surah Al-Anbiya — MECCAN**   | Late Meccan                          |
| Cain, Abel, and the crow (5:27-32)                | Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer; Mishnah Sanhedrin | **Surah Al-Ma'idah — MEDINAN** | Medinan (this one fits the timeline) |
| Queen of Sheba / Solomon / hoopoe (27:15-44)      | Targum Sheni                              | **Surah An-Naml — MECCAN**     | Meccan                               |
| Mary at the Temple / Zachariah (3:35-37, 19:1-15) | Protevangelium of James                   | **Surah Maryam — MECCAN**      | Early-to-mid Meccan                  |

**The Abraham and idols story** — one of the most frequently cited "borrowings" — appears in Surah 21 (Al-Anbiya), classified with **95% confidence** as late Meccan. Muhammad had not yet set foot in Medina. He had no documented sustained contact with any Jewish scholarly community.

**The Solomon and Queen of Sheba narrative** with the hoopoe bird appears in Surah 27 (An-Naml), also Meccan.

**The Mary/Zachariah temple narrative** paralleling the Protevangelium of James appears in Surah 19 (Maryam), an early-to-mid Meccan surah.

If Muhammad learned this material from Medina Jews, how did it get into surahs revealed years before he ever lived among them?

### 3b. Possible Counter-Arguments

**Counter-argument 1:** "He may have encountered individual Jews in Mecca or during trade journeys."

**Response:** Mecca was a polytheistic trading town. While isolated Jewish or Christian individuals may have passed through, no source documents a sustained relationship between Muhammad and any Jewish scholar in Mecca. The Quraysh themselves accused Muhammad of being "taught by a human" (Qur'an 16:103), but the Qur'an's response notes that the person they pointed to spoke a foreign language — and no one could name a consistent teacher. If there were a known Jewish informant, the Quraysh — who had every motive to discredit Muhammad — would have named him.

**Counter-argument 2:** "Stories circulated through oral culture."

**Response:** General narrative awareness (Abraham was a monotheist, Moses confronted Pharaoh) could circulate orally. But the Qur'an's engagement goes far beyond general awareness — it includes specialist Talmudic legal principles (Mishnah Sanhedrin 4:5), specific Midrashic narrative details, and systematic corrections of these traditions. The empirical science of oral transmission (Bartlett 1932, Vansina 1961, Rubin 1995) shows that oral retelling degrades information — it does not enhance it into more precise, more systematic, and more theologically sophisticated forms.

## 4. SPECIFIC TALMUDIC/MIDRASHIC PARALLELS IN THE QUR'AN

### 4a. Abraham and the Idols

**Qur'anic version (21:51-70):** Abraham confronts his father and people about idol worship. He smashes the idols except the largest, then challenges his people to "ask it" if it can speak. When they admit idols cannot speak, Abraham uses this as proof of their folly. They throw him into a fire, and God commands the fire to be "coolness and safety" for Abraham.

**Midrashic version (Genesis Rabbah 38:13):** Abraham, working in his father Terah's idol shop, smashes the idols and places the hammer in the largest one's hand. He tells Terah the big idol destroyed the others. When Terah says "they have no understanding," Abraham responds: "Do your ears hear what your mouth is saying?"

**Key differences:**

- The Qur'an includes an elaborate dialogue structure and argumentation pattern not found in the Midrash
- The fire episode is in the Qur'an but its details differ from Talmudic versions
- The Qur'anic version lacks the idol-shop setting of the Midrash
- The theological framing is entirely different — the Qur'an uses the episode to establish Abraham's prophetic mission, not as a rabbinic anecdote

**Dating problem:** The manuscripts of Genesis Rabbah are late and divergent. The earliest known manuscript (Vatican 30) dates 400-500 years **after** the advent of Islam. The text underwent continuous expansion and redaction, making reconstruction of any "original" 5th-century version impossible.

### 4b. Cain, Abel, and the Crow Teaching Burial

**Qur'anic version (5:31):** "Then God sent a crow scratching up the ground to show him how to hide the shame of his brother."

**Talmudic version (Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer 21):** "The Holy One, blessed be He, sent him two clean birds, and one killed the other. Then he dug with his claws and buried him before their eyes."

**Key differences:**

- In the Qur'an, Cain learns from the crow. In Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer, it is Adam (not Cain) who learns.
- The Qur'an specifies a single crow; the Talmudic version involves two birds.
- The contexts and theological purposes differ entirely.

**Critical dating problem:** Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer is **post-Islamic**. Leopold Zunz dated it to ~729 CE. The Jewish Encyclopedia dates it to the "beginning of the 9th century," noting it references "the three stages of the Mohammadan conquest" and mentions "Fatima and Ayesha." Modern scholars Strack and Stemberger place it in the "eighth or ninth century." The earliest citation is by Pirkoi Ben Baboi in the 9th century — at least 200 years after Muhammad's death.

**This means the Qur'an CANNOT have borrowed from Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer. The influence, if any, ran the other direction.**

### 4c. "Whoever Saves a Life..." (Qur'an 5:32 // Mishnah Sanhedrin 4:5)

**Qur'anic version (5:32):** "Whoever kills a soul — unless for murder or spreading corruption in the land — it is as if he killed all mankind; and whoever saves one, it is as if he saved all mankind."

**Mishnah version (Sanhedrin 4:5):** "Whoever destroys a soul of Israel, Scripture considers him as if he had destroyed a whole world. And whoever saves a soul of Israel, Scripture considers him as if he had saved a whole world."

**Key differences:**

1. **Scope:** The Mishnah restricts the principle to "a soul **of Israel**." The Qur'an universalizes it to all of humanity — any soul, Muslim or non-Muslim.
2. **Exception clause:** The Qur'an adds "unless for murder or spreading corruption in the land" — absent from the Mishnah.
3. **Context:** The Mishnah uses this in the context of witness intimidation in capital cases. The Qur'an uses it as a universal moral principle following the Cain and Abel narrative.
4. **The Qur'an explicitly attributes this to divine decree for the Children of Israel** ("Because of that, We decreed upon the Children of Israel..."), framing it as a reminder of what God told them — not as something learned from them.

**The Qur'an's explicit framing:** Note that 5:32 begins: "Because of that [the Cain and Abel story], **We decreed** upon the Children of Israel..." The Qur'an claims this principle as divine decree, not human wisdom. It says God told the Israelites this — and it is now reminding them and correcting their restriction of it to Israelite souls only.

### 4d. Solomon, the Queen of Sheba, and the Hoopoe (27:15-44)

**Qur'anic version:** Solomon commands an army of jinn, humans, and birds. The hoopoe bird brings news of a queen who worships the sun. Solomon sends a letter; the queen comes to visit. She encounters a glass floor she mistakes for water.

**Targum Sheni (Second Targum of Esther):** Contains remarkably similar elements — Solomon commands birds, threatens the hoopoe for absence, the hoopoe brings news of a distant queen, and the glass-floor episode appears.

**Dating problem:** The Targum Sheni reached its "final form" around 700-800 CE — **after Islam's emergence**. The earliest manuscript evidence dates to the 12th century CE. The Encyclopaedia Judaica notes the Targum Sheni "also made use of Arabic sources." Edward Ullendorff concedes the Qur'an may preserve this narrative earliest.

### 4e. The Mountain Held Over Israel (2:63, 7:171 // Shabbat 88a)

**Qur'anic version (7:171):** "And when We raised the mountain above them as if it were a dark cloud and they were certain that it would fall upon them..."

**Talmudic version (Shabbat 88a):** God held Mount Sinai over the heads of the Israelites "like a barrel" and threatened to bury them if they did not accept the Torah.

**Key difference:** The Qur'an mentions this as a historical event within its narrative; the Talmud presents it as aggadic (homiletical) interpretation. The Qur'an does not frame it as an interpretation of an existing text — it presents it as what happened.

### 4f. Mary at the Temple / Zachariah

**Qur'anic version (3:35-37, 19:1-15):** Mary is dedicated to the Temple by her mother. Zachariah is appointed her guardian (determined by casting lots). He finds miraculous provision with her. He then prays for a child and is granted John (Yahya).

**Protevangelium of James (2nd century CE):** Contains strikingly similar elements — Mary's dedication to the Temple, miraculous feeding, the casting of lots for her guardian.

**Note:** This parallel is with a **Christian** apocryphal text, not a Jewish one — undermining the "learned from Jews" hypothesis for this content. The Protevangelium circulated primarily in Syriac Christian communities, not Jewish ones.

## 5. THE CORRECTION PATTERN: NOT BORROWING BUT CORRECTIVE RETELLING

### 5a. The Core Distinction

The borrowing hypothesis assumes the Qur'an _repeats_ Jewish material. But careful analysis reveals it _corrects_ Jewish material — systematically, consistently, and in ways that no Jewish teacher would have taught.

### 5b. The Golden Calf: The Most Striking Correction

| Element            | Bible (Exodus 32)              | Jewish Tradition                    | Qur'an (20:85-97)                           |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------ | ----------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| Who made the calf? | **Aaron**                      | Aaron, under pressure               | **Al-Samiri** (a previously unknown figure) |
| Aaron's role       | Built the calf, made the altar | Complicit, varying degrees          | **Opposed the calf**, warned the people     |
| Aaron's character  | Compromised                    | Partially excused in some Midrashim | **Completely exonerated** as a prophet      |

**Michael Pregill** (Oxford University Press, 2020) in _The Golden Calf between Bible and Qur'an_ demonstrates that:

- The Qur'anic version is "an intentional, sophisticated, and largely original reading and interpretive retelling" of the Biblical story
- The Jewish sources scholars cited as Qur'anic sources actually **postdate the Qur'an** and may derive from Islamic elaborations
- The al-Samiri figure has **no known precedent** in any Jewish, Christian, or Samaritan source

**The critical question:** Would Jewish teachers teach Muhammad that their own scripture is wrong about Aaron? Would they teach him to exonerate Aaron — contradicting their own Torah — and blame a figure they had never heard of? This is not learning from a tradition; it is overruling a tradition.

### 5c. Prophetic Infallibility: Systematic Removal of Prophetic Sins

The Bible attributes major sins to prophets. The Qur'an systematically removes them:

| Prophet     | Biblical Account                                            | Qur'anic Account                                                           |
| ----------- | ----------------------------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Noah**    | Gets drunk, lies naked (Genesis 9:20-21)                    | Righteous prophet, no such episode                                         |
| **David**   | Adultery with Bathsheba, murders Uriah (2 Samuel 11)        | Righteous king; minor lapse of judgment in a dispute, immediately repented |
| **Solomon** | Idolatry in old age, worships foreign gods (1 Kings 11:1-8) | **Never** participated in idolatry; a righteous prophet throughout         |
| **Lot**     | Drunken incest with daughters (Genesis 19:30-38)            | Righteous prophet who fled Sodom; no such episode                          |
| **Aaron**   | Built the golden calf (Exodus 32)                           | Opposed the calf; exonerated                                               |

**The pattern is not random.** It is theologically systematic: the Qur'an maintains the doctrine of prophetic infallibility (_ismah_) — that prophets do not commit major sins. This directly contradicts the Biblical/Jewish theological framework where prophetic fallibility serves to demonstrate human weakness and divine grace.

**Could Muhammad have learned prophetic infallibility FROM Jewish teachers?** No. This doctrine contradicts their own scripture. Jewish theology is built partly on the humanity and fallibility of its prophets. No Jewish teacher would teach that their own patriarchs never sinned — because their own Torah says they did.

### 5d. The "Chosen People" Correction

The Qur'an acknowledges that the Children of Israel were once "chosen" (favored) — but redefines chosenness:

- **Jewish theology:** Chosenness is unconditional and eternal, linked to ethnic identity
- **Qur'anic theology:** Chosenness was **conditional** on covenant faithfulness and **functional** (serving as host nation of prophets), not ethnic privilege. It ended with Muhammad's prophethood. "The noblest of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you" (49:13).

Would Jewish scholars teach Muhammad to reject the central organizing principle of their own theology? Would they teach him that chosenness is conditional, has ended, and that righteousness alone — not descent from Abraham — determines divine favor?

## 6. ABRAHAM GEIGER AND THE "BORROWING" TRADITION

### 6a. Geiger's Work

Abraham Geiger (1810-1874), the founding father of Reform Judaism, wrote _Was hat Mohammed aus dem Judenthume aufgenommen?_ ("What Did Muhammad Borrow from Judaism?") in 1833. He identified 14+ terms and several concepts he argued Muhammad took from Hebrew Bible and rabbinic literature.

### 6b. Geiger's Method and Its Problems

1. **Parallelomania:** Geiger sometimes posited Qur'anic borrowings from texts without verifying their chronology. He cited Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer as a Qur'anic source without realizing it was **composed after Islam** (8th-9th century CE).

2. **Naivety:** Later Arabists concluded his analysis "lacked sophistication" and examined "inadequate primary sources."

3. **Selective evidence:** Geiger could account for some Jewish parallels but could not explain:
   - The Christian content in the Qur'an (Protevangelium of James parallels, etc.)
   - The original content with no known source (al-Samiri, Haman as Egyptian, etc.)
   - The systematic corrections of Jewish tradition
   - The Egyptian, Persian, and Greek content

4. **Motivated scholarship:** Geiger wrote this as a prize essay at age 23. The very framing of the question ("What did Muhammad borrow?") presupposes borrowing before investigating the evidence.

### 6c. Norman Stillman's Caution

Norman Stillman, a specialist in Jewish-Arab history, warned: "One should be extremely cautious about assigning specific origins to the story discussed here — or for that matter, any other story in the Qur'an." He noted Geiger's work gave an "exaggerated view" of Jewish influence.

## 7. THE DATING CRISIS: WHEN WERE THE "SOURCES" ACTUALLY WRITTEN?

This is one of the most damaging problems for the borrowing thesis. Many texts cited as Qur'anic "sources" **postdate the Qur'an**:

| Claimed "Source"                 | Claimed Dating              | Actual Dating Evidence                                                                            | Post-Islamic?                                                                                 |
| -------------------------------- | --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer**       | "Ancient"                   | 8th-9th century CE; references Islamic conquest, Fatima, Ayesha                                   | **YES**                                                                                       |
| **Targum Sheni** (Solomon/Sheba) | Pre-Islamic                 | Final form ~700-800 CE; earliest MS 12th century; uses Arabic sources                             | **YES**                                                                                       |
| **Targum Pseudo-Jonathan**       | Pre-Islamic                 | Final form 7th-8th century or later                                                               | **LIKELY**                                                                                    |
| **Midrash Tanhuma**              | Pre-Islamic                 | Compiled 9th century CE                                                                           | **YES**                                                                                       |
| **Genesis Rabbah**               | ~5th century                | Earliest MS (Vatican 30) is 400-500 years post-Islam; underwent continuous post-Islamic redaction | **PARTIALLY** — core may be pre-Islamic but manuscripts are post-Islamic and text is unstable |
| **Mishnah Sanhedrin**            | ~200 CE (Mishnah redaction) | Earlier, but underwent later censorship affecting key phrases                                     | **No** — but oral transmission path undocumented                                              |

**The Encyclopaedia of Islam notes:** "The Qur'an appears to present the earliest record of certain midrashic developments."

**The implication:** In several cases, the direction of influence may be **reversed** — the Qur'an may be the source that later Jewish compilations drew upon, not the other way around. At minimum, the chronological evidence does not support the simple "Muhammad borrowed from Jewish texts" narrative.

## 8. ORAL TRANSMISSION FEASIBILITY

### 8a. The Transmission Chain Problem

For Talmudic material to reach Muhammad, the following chain would need to work:

1. A Babylonian rabbi, trained in Aramaic/Hebrew Talmudic discourse in Iraq, would need to...
2. Travel ~1,500 km to the Hijaz, then...
3. Translate complex rabbinic legal and exegetical material from Aramaic/Hebrew into Arabic, then...
4. Transmit it orally to an Arabic-speaking illiterate merchant, who would then...
5. Not merely repeat it but _correct_ it, _universalize_ it, and _systematize_ it into a coherent theological framework contradicting the source tradition.

No historical evidence documents any step of this chain.

### 8b. What Empirical Science Says About Oral Transmission

The empirical science of oral transmission (Bartlett 1932, Vansina 1961, Rubin 1995) consistently shows:

| Oral Transmission Prediction                        | What the Qur'an Actually Does                                                            |
| --------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Details are lost                                    | Details are added and made more precise                                                  |
| Narratives shorten and simplify                     | Narratives are restructured with new theological frameworks                              |
| Unfamiliar elements are replaced with familiar ones | Unfamiliar Talmudic concepts are preserved and deployed                                  |
| Theological complexity degrades                     | Theological complexity increases (systematic prophetic infallibility, universal ethics)  |
| Cultural assimilation occurs                        | Counter-cultural content is maintained (correcting both Jewish AND Christian traditions) |

**The Qur'an moves in the opposite direction of oral degradation.** If it arrived through oral transmission, it is the only documented case in the history of oral transmission science where information became more precise, more complex, and more systematic through retelling.

### 8c. The Multi-Tradition Problem

The Qur'an does not engage with Jewish sources alone. It simultaneously engages:

- Hebrew Biblical material
- Aramaic Talmudic/Midrashic material
- Syriac Christian apocrypha (Protevangelium of James, Infancy Gospel of Thomas)
- Egyptian historical knowledge (Pharaoh/King distinction, Haman as Egyptian)
- Persian traditions (Dhul-Qarnayn / Alexander narratives)
- Greek philosophical concepts
- Pre-Islamic Arabian religion

An illiterate merchant in a trading town would need access to **all** of these traditions simultaneously, which requires institutional scholarly infrastructure that did not exist in Mecca or Medina.

## 9. WHAT JEWISH TEACHERS WOULD NEVER TEACH

The Qur'an contains theological positions that no Jewish teacher would transmit, because they directly contradict Jewish theology:

1. **Prophetic infallibility** — contradicts the Torah's own narratives of Noah, David, Solomon, Lot, and Aaron sinning
2. **Rejection of ethnic chosenness** — dismantles the theological foundation of Jewish identity
3. **Aaron's exoneration** — directly contradicts Exodus 32
4. **Ishmael's elevation** — the Qur'an treats Ishmael as a righteous prophet and co-builder of the Ka'bah with Abraham, contradicting Jewish tradition's marginalization of Ishmael
5. **Supersessionism** — the Qur'an claims to supersede the Torah and correct Jewish corruption of the original message
6. **Accusations of scriptural falsification** (_tahrif_) — the Qur'an accuses Jews of corrupting their own scripture
7. **Muhammad as the final prophet** — fulfilling and closing the prophetic lineage that Jews believe remains open

**The logical problem:** The borrowing hypothesis requires Jewish teachers to have taught Muhammad material that:

- Contradicts their own scripture
- Undermines their own theology
- Accuses them of corruption
- Elevates the rival lineage of Ishmael over Isaac
- Claims their covenant has been superseded

This is not learning from a tradition. It is dismantling a tradition from a position of claimed superior authority. No documented case exists of a religious community voluntarily teaching an outsider to refute their own core beliefs.

## 10. THE MODERN SCHOLARLY PARADIGM SHIFT

### 10a. From "Borrowing" to "Intertextuality"

Contemporary Western Qur'anic studies has undergone what scholars describe as a **paradigm shift**. The old model (Geiger 1833, Tisdall 1905) treated the Qur'an as a "derivative work comprised of confused adaptations of biblical and extra-biblical lore." The new model treats it as an autonomous text engaged in sophisticated dialogue with earlier traditions.

Key scholars in this shift:

**Sidney Griffith** (Catholic University of America): "The Qur'an cannot be reduced to any presumed sources. Earlier discourses appear in it not only in a new setting, but shaped, trimmed and re-formulated for an essentially new narrative."

**Angelika Neuwirth** (Free University of Berlin): The Qur'an was communicated to "listeners whose education already comprised biblical and post-biblical lore," providing "answers to the questions raised in biblical exegesis." She characterizes certain Qur'anic passages as "intertextual corrective commentary" on Jewish and Christian creedal statements.

**Gabriel Said Reynolds** (University of Notre Dame): The Qur'an should be interpreted in light of earlier scripture because it "depends on the Biblical knowledge of its audience." But this dependence is not derivative — the Qur'an characteristically engages with the Christian version of shared accounts, suggesting a more nuanced engagement than mere borrowing.

**Holger Zellentin** (University of Tubingen): The Qur'an shares a "legal culture" with late antique Jewish-Christian communities, particularly as reflected in the Didascalia Apostolorum (3rd century CE). But this is a shared milieu, not direct textual dependence: "the Qur'an is not 'based' on the Didascalia in any direct way."

**Michael Pregill** (UCLA): The Qur'anic golden calf narrative is "an intentional, sophisticated, and largely original reading and interpretive retelling" of the Biblical story, not a confused borrowing. Jewish sources scholars cited as Qur'anic sources actually postdate the Qur'an.

### 10b. The Yaqeen Institute Framework

The Yaqeen Institute's comprehensive paper on Qur'anic intertextuality provides a seven-category analytical framework:

1. **Early extra-biblical traditions** — motifs from the Second Temple period preserved in later texts; the Qur'an may access the underlying tradition, not the specific late text
2. **Conscious biblical departures** — deliberate corrections with cross-linguistic wordplay (e.g., Hebrew-Arabic phonetic inversions in the golden calf narrative)
3. **Coincidental overlap** — universal themes (God supports prophets) independently expressed
4. **Shared legal traditions** — parallel legal principles arising from a common divine source
5. **Counter-narrative strategy** — deliberate correction of legendary/mythological accounts (e.g., Moses replacing Alexander in the fish narrative)
6. **Post-Qur'anic redaction** — "sources" that actually postdate the Qur'an (Targum Sheni, Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer)
7. **Unstable manuscript traditions** — texts whose manuscripts are so late and divergent that priority cannot be assigned

### 10c. Griffith on Syriac Christian Sources

Sidney Griffith argues that Syriac Biblical commentary — not Jewish Talmudic material — is "in all likelihood the immediate source of biblical lore for Muhammad and the Qur'an." This shifts the conversation from Jewish to Christian intermediaries. But even Griffith does not endorse simple borrowing; he emphasizes the Qur'an's radical reshaping of everything it engages.

## 11. VERDICT: WEIGHING BOTH SIDES

### What the Evidence Supports FOR the "Jewish Contact" Hypothesis:

1. Jewish communities existed in Medina, and Muhammad interacted with them
2. Some parallels between Qur'anic content and Jewish traditions do exist
3. A _bayt al-midras_ (house of study) reportedly existed in Medina
4. General awareness of Biblical narratives circulated in late antique Arabia
5. Some Medinan-period content (e.g., Qur'an 5:32 and Mishnah Sanhedrin 4:5) could theoretically reflect contact

### What the Evidence Shows AGAINST the "Jewish Contact" Hypothesis:

1. **Timeline failure:** Most Talmudic parallels appear in MECCAN surahs, revealed before any sustained Jewish contact
2. **Dating reversal:** Several "source" texts (Pirke de-Rabbi Eliezer, Targum Sheni, Midrash Tanhuma, Targum Pseudo-Jonathan) postdate the Qur'an
3. **No Talmudic academy in Arabia:** The scholarly infrastructure required for transmitting specialist rabbinic material did not exist in the Hijaz
4. **Language barrier:** Medina Jews spoke Arabic; the Talmud is in Hebrew/Aramaic
5. **Correction, not repetition:** The Qur'an systematically corrects Jewish tradition in ways no Jewish teacher would teach
6. **Anti-Jewish theology:** The Qur'an rejects ethnic chosenness, prophetic fallibility, and Aaron's guilt — core Jewish positions
7. **Multi-tradition engagement:** The Qur'an engages not just Jewish but Christian, Egyptian, Persian, and Greek traditions simultaneously — Jewish contact alone cannot explain this
8. **Oral transmission impossibility:** Empirical science shows oral transmission degrades information; the Qur'an shows enhancement
9. **Original content:** Material like al-Samiri, the Pharaoh/King distinction, and Haman's Egyptian identity has no known source in any tradition
10. **Scholarly paradigm shift:** Contemporary academics have moved from "borrowing" to "intertextual engagement" — recognizing the Qur'an as an autonomous text, not a derivative one

### The Bottom Line

The hypothesis that 13 years of contact with Medina's Jewish communities explains the Qur'an's Talmudic/Midrashic content faces multiple empirical failures:

- It cannot explain the Meccan-period parallels (timeline problem)
- It cannot explain the corrections of Jewish tradition (correction problem)
- It cannot explain the post-Qur'anic dating of many "source" texts (chronology problem)
- It cannot explain the multi-traditional engagement (scope problem)
- It cannot explain the original content with no known source (originality problem)
- It cannot explain how oral transmission produced enhanced rather than degraded material (mechanism problem)

Even at its strongest, the hypothesis can account for only a fraction of the Qur'an's engagement with earlier traditions — and even that fraction raises more questions than it answers. The Qur'an does not behave like a text assembled from borrowed material. It behaves like a text that knows these traditions from a position of authority and corrects them with surgical precision.

As Sidney Griffith summarizes: the Qur'an takes earlier material and presents it "not only in a new setting, but shaped, trimmed and re-formulated for an essentially new narrative." The question is not whether the Qur'an engages earlier traditions — it obviously does. The question is whether an illiterate merchant in 7th-century Arabia could have accessed, processed, corrected, and systematized material from 7+ linguistic and cultural traditions through brief contact with an arabized Jewish community that had no documented connection to the Talmudic academies.

The evidence says no.
