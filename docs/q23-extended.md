SECTION H — QUR’AN TRANSMISSION, QIRA’AT, MANUSCRIPTS, AND PRESERVATION

Purpose of this section:

This section distinguishes between:

- authorial drafting,
- scribal correction,
- orthographic variation,
- canonical qira’at,
- non-canonical manuscript variation,
- and preservation architecture.

These categories are not equivalent.
The mere existence of one does not automatically prove the others.

This section is meant to prevent category confusion.
In particular, it prevents the common fallacy of moving from:
“there are variants / qira’at / manuscript corrections”
to
“therefore the Qur’an underwent an ordinary human drafting or editorial process.”

That does not follow automatically.

Recommended questions:

H1. Has any manuscript evidence ever demonstrated an authorial drafting or revision process behind the Qur’anic text?

Why this matters:
This is the real target behind earlier drafting questions. The issue is not whether any manuscript ever shows irregularity, but whether any physical evidence demonstrates an ordinary human compositional drafting process behind the Qur’an.

Recommended role:
[CRITICAL] [FOUNDATIONAL]

---

H2. Do accepted qira’at differences, by themselves, constitute evidence of ordinary human drafting or compositional revision?

Why this matters:
Canonical qira’at are part of the transmitted Islamic model and should not be casually conflated with evidence of ordinary human authorship or later authorial rewriting.

Recommended role:
regular

---

H3. Do ordinary scribal corrections, orthographic adjustments, or manuscript erasures, by themselves, prove an authorial drafting process?

Why this matters:
Scribal behavior and authorial composition are not the same category. A manuscript correction or erasure does not automatically establish that the text was undergoing human drafting in the ordinary literary sense.

Recommended role:
regular

---

H4. Are the canonical qira’at already integrated within the traditional Islamic transmission model rather than functioning as late hostile discoveries against it?

Why this matters:
This prevents the false framing that qira’at are modern disruptions to the Islamic account rather than part of the transmitted recitational tradition itself.

Recommended role:
regular

---

H5. Do early manuscript variants necessarily overturn the claim that the Qur’an is exceptionally preserved at the level this protocol intends?

Why this matters:
Preservation does not need to mean “no variation of any kind ever existed.” The protocol should clarify what level of preservation is being claimed and whether limited textual variation actually defeats that claim.

Recommended role:
evaluative

---

H6. Can the existence of limited manuscript or recitational variation, by itself, explain the Qur’an’s literary, structural, and delivery profile as a human-authored text?

Why this matters:
Even if some variation exists, that does not automatically solve the larger authorship question. This question reconnects transmission issues to the broader model-comparison framework.

Recommended role:
evaluative or [CRITICAL] if desired

H7. Is there evidence that any manuscript variation reflects a sustained hidden editorial project capable of generating the Qur’an’s full form post hoc?

Why this matters:
This directly tests stronger editorial rescue models. Limited manuscript variation is one thing; evidence of a hidden project capable of producing the Qur’an’s total form is another.

Recommended role:
[CRITICAL]

H8. Does distinguishing between accepted qira’at, scribal phenomena, and authorial drafting reduce category confusion in textual criticism of the Qur’an?

Why this matters:

SECTION I — DISTRIBUTED MEMORIZATION AND PRESERVATION ARCHITECTURE

Purpose of this section:

This section concerns preservation architecture, not merely manuscript survival.
The issue is not only whether written copies exist, but whether the Qur’an is preserved through a uniquely redundant combination of written transmission and mass distributed living memorization.

This should not be framed casually as “blockchain” inside the formal scored questions, since that metaphor is vivid but technically distractible. If used at all, “blockchain” should appear only in commentary, not in the formal scored wording.

Recommended questions:

I1. If all written copies of the Qur’an were destroyed, does the living memorization tradition plausibly preserve enough of the text to reconstruct it word-for-word at scale?

Why this matters:
This tests whether the Qur’an is preserved only in objects or also in a distributed living transmission network.

Recommended role:
evaluative or evaluative-critical

---

I2. If the same destruction test were applied to major books generally, would most be recoverable word-for-word from living mass memorization alone?

Why this matters:
This creates a comparative baseline. Most books are preserved mainly through external copies, not through mass living memorization.

Recommended role:
regular comparative

---

I3. Does the Qur’an’s preservation through both written transmission and mass distributed memorization constitute an unusually robust preservation architecture compared to ordinary books?

Why this matters:
This is the clean summary question. It avoids overstating “mostly preserved in memory” while still identifying the unusual strength of the Qur’an’s oral-plus-written preservation network.

Recommended role:
evaluative

---

I4. Does this preservation architecture make large-scale undetected post hoc editing materially less plausible than it would be for an ordinary text preserved mainly through manuscripts alone?

Why this matters:
This directly pressures editorial and post-hoc revision models. A text preserved through mass cross-checking memorization is harder to alter undetectably than one preserved mainly through manuscript transmission alone.

Recommended role:
[CRITICAL] or evaluative-critical
